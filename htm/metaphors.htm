Metaphors in Our Language

One of the things we take for granted everyday are the metaphors that we use. Metaphors relate something to another thing because of the beliefs we hold about those items being related. Some of these metaphors are directly related to the ways that we view the world. So it would make sense that the Five Element Theory has even had an impact upon the language which is used in everyday life. The same thing has taken place in Western culture though related to the Four Humors which was a dominate belief through the medieval ages.

The easiest place to start with looking at the Five Element Theory's impact on language is in the way we express anger. Sometimes anger is an emotion which is grouped together with the element of fire. Other things that tend to be in the fire element grouping are the color red, the heart, and blood. So it is no wonder that many Chinese sayings used to depict anger will instead reference these different ideas all grouped under the element of fire.

However, the more common approach is for anger to be grouped as part of the wood element. The wood element also relates to things such as the color green, the liver, the gall blader, windy climates, and the eyes. This makes sense when you see Chinese saying of anger which relate to the liver, eyes, and gas.

One reason to explain both the use of fire and wood as elements related to anger is that the element of wood promotes fire. In this way something that effects the body in terms of wood is going to produce an effect from fire as well. In this way we find that both fire and wood can be used in metaphors relating to anger in China. This is a large impact which the Five Element Theory has on culture that is hard to find because it is taken for granted in society.

Chinese Sayings

The following is a list of some Chinese sayings and their translation into English. These were found in Ning Yu's paper, "Metaphorical Expressions Of Anger And Happiness In English And Chinese", which looked at the use of different symbols in emotional expression.

Wo qi de lian-shang huo-lala de.
I got so angry that my face was peppery hot.
Ta qi de cui-huzi deng-yanjing.
He was so angry he was blowing his moustache and opening his eyes wide.
Ta qi-huhu de.
He's puffing and blowing with gas.
Ta gan-huo hen wang.
He's got a roaring fire in his liver.
Ta xin-tou huo qi.
Fire started to flare up in his heart.
Ta xin-zhong you qi.
He has gas in his heart.
